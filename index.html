Electrolytes : An Overview

An explanation of what electrolytes are, their uses in the body, and the best replenishment methods for them.

What are electrolytes?

Electrolytes are substances that when dissolved in water dissociate into cations and anions, thus producing a solution that can conduct electricity. The dissociation does not lean towards more cations or more anions, so the solution remains neutral. Dissociation is when molecules break up into smaller parts, and in the situation with electrolytes, particularly ions.

The strength of the electrolyte is extrapolated from the amount of dissociation that occurs from the electrolyte being added to water. So, the more ions in the solution after the electrolyte is added, the stronger the electrolyte is. If no dissociation occurs, then the substance is not an ion.

Electrolytes in the Human Body

Some common electrolytes in the human body include calcium, sodium, potassium, chloride, magnesium, and bicarbonate. Each of these electrolytes seves its own purpose for the human body. Most fall into the similar category of maintaining equilibrium with the solution that the cells are in to the cells themselves.

Sodium, or Na+, is an electrolyte that is obtained mostly from table salt. Sodium helps regulate the osmosis that occurs in cells to regulate the overall amount of water in the body. Also, since the nervous system requires electricity to signal for communication, sodium is important for the generation of the signals.

Chloride, just like sodium is obtained from the consumption of table salt. It helps to maintain a balance with most fluids of the body and is usually found in the outside of cells or blood. It also plays a vital role in keeping the body at the right pH level.

Potassium is an important cation for the body. The functions of potassium include keeping a regular heartbeat and helping with muscle function. Potassium also plays a role for nueral communication.

Magnesium plays a critical role in the creation of both RNA and DNA. It boosts the immune system, and helps maintain normal functions of pretty much everything in your body. It even takes a part of over 300 biochemical reactions!

Both calcium and phosphorous work to strengthen the bones and teeth. Phosphorous is crucial for energy production at the cellular level while calcium is necessary for blood clots. Phosphorous is also necessary for the growth and repair of tissues.

Bicarbonate is hugely vital in the controlling of pH levels in the body. It is used as a buffer for the acidity of blood and other bodily fluids. Bicarbonate itself is an alkaline solution, so when the body is getting acidic, more of it is released. When it is heading towards more alkalinity, less will be released.
